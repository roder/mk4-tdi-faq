How to install new timing belt on Jetta, New Beetle,Golf (ALH engine 1998-2003)

This article shows how to install the new timing belt on the TDI engine and final adjustments on the injection pump.

Procedure

The camshaft lock bar (and its feeler gauges/business cards), crankshaft lock, and injection pump pin lock should all be in place.

If you removed the engine mount instead of working around it, you know how tricky it can be to reinstall it.  Loosely put it in position so that it stays out of the way and you can work around it until you get the belt and rollers on.  After you get the rollers and belt on, loosely hand tighten the mount in position.

Install a new tensioner pulley.  The two holes for the spanner wrench should be at the bottom.  Make sure the tensioner's tab (called tensioning roller retaining lug in the Bentley service manual) is inserted into its alignment slot (indicated below with red arrow and in more pictures below).  Tighten the 13mm nut loosely finger tight only.  The purpose of finger tight only is so that when you tighten the tensioner in a later step, the camshaft and injection pump sprockets can rotate, spreading the tension evenly across the timing belt (the cam lock and injection pump lock should be in place so that although their shafts are locked, their sprockets are be free to rotate).

Here is a picture of the tab not in its slot (pic by rallysmurf).  It should not look like this.

The large roller uses a 16mm stretch bolt, you should replace the roller and bolt with new ones, tighten to 30 ft lbs + 1/4 turn.  Also change the small rollers and water pump.  Torque specs also at the bottom of the page.

Put the new belt on.  Note the belt routing.  Slip the belt over the camshaft sprocket and then slide the camshaft sprocket onto the taper.  The sprocket and taper surface should be clean and free of oil or grease.

Put the 19mm camshaft sprocket bolt on and firmly hand tighten the bolt so that you know the sprocket is on correctly and flat on the taper.  Then loosen the bolt about 1 or 2 turns so that there is no resistance to letting it rotate.  Wiggle and rotate the camshaft sprocket to make sure it's flat and can rotate on the taper freely.  This is to let it rotate when you tension the belt.

Again, the camshaft lock bar holds the camshaft in place while the camshaft sprocket can rotate independently - this is why you need to use the camshaft lock bar and need to loosen the sprocket!  There is no missing key on the camshaft, the sprocket is a tapered/conical fit to the camshaft end.  It's also difficult to put the new belt on unless the camshaft sprocket is off.

The injection pump bolts should already be loose (3x 13mm bolts).  Your replacement bolts should be multiple use bolts and should not be single use stretch bolts.  The kits sold by the vendors linked in "timing belt part 1" do not use stretch bolts.  If they are pointed at the end and are tapered at the neck, they are single use only bolts. (see below illustration).  If they are 1 use only bolts, remove them 1 at a time and hand thread the replacements back.  Do not tighten yet.

The point of loosening the 3 bolts is to fine tune the injection timing later.  Remember, the sprocket should rotate independently of the pump while you tighten the tensioner.  The lock pin only locks the injection pump position and guarantees that the car will start and has approximate timing, you should use a VCDS to fine tune the injection timing after the belt is reinstalled.

If you didn't double check the position of the injection lock pin before, confirm its position now.  Remember, the hole should be bracketed by the slot.  I suggest rotating the injection pump sprocket so that the oval hole is somewhere in the middle of its play so that you have room to adjust it later (as shown below).

The sprocket should be positioned so that the bolts and oval holes are somewhere in the middle.  This gives you the maximum adjustment range when fine tuning it later.

Again, the camshaft and injection pump should be locked but their sprockets should be free to rotate when you tighten the tensioner.  Use the 2587 2 pin spanner wrench to tighten the tensioner clockwise in the direction of the blue arrow until the index marks (boxes pictured below) line up.  If you have an automatic transmission, the pointer should rotate into the middle of the "V".  

CAUTION - Although it's possible to tighten the tensioner in a counterclockwise direction, this is wrong, only tighten in a clockwise direction!   The engine should be cold when setting the tensioner.  It automatically adjusts to temperature so don't be surprised if it's slightly off when later checking it on a warm engine.  

The raised dash and the notch highlighted below will come into alignment as you tighten the tensioner clockwise with the spanner wrench.  As you turn the tensioner clockwise, the camshaft sprocket, injection pump sprocket, and water pump should rotate slightly.  This is shown in the below videos.  Remember, the camshaft/pump sprocket bolts are loose so that they can rotate and spread tension across the belt evenly.  This is what it looks like after you tighten it - the alignment marks are facing each other after turning the tensioner clockwise and the tensioner slot is still in its tab.  Remember, since you're viewing the tensioner at an angle, beware of parallax.  Use a mirror to confirm that it's at the correct position and that the tab is in its slot.  If it's correct, tighten the tensioner's nut (13mm) to 15 ft lbs.   

Caution: the Bentley service manual calls the tensioner nut a "lock nut" in 2 places but doesn't call it a lock nut in the diagram.  If you want, you can put a light dab of blue/light strength threadlocker on the stud.  Don't get threadlocker everywhere - more is not always better.  Also note that threadlocker changes torque specs, refer to your product's spec sheet for details on how to adjust the torque spec.  I would just stick to 15 ft-lb since 1 ft-lb overtightened will not damage it or interfere with operation of the tensioner.  Again, this website provides tips and an interpretation of the service manual but is not responsible for the work that you do to your car.

Here is a video showing a closeup of the tensioner and a video of the belt.  Click the play button (the triangle that points to the right) to view the video.  The button to the right of "HQ" will switch the video to full screen.  Remember, it's normal for the sprockets to rotate when you tighten the belt.  The crankshaft sprocket should not move since you are locking its position.

Tighten the 3x 13mm injection pump bolts.  Once the tensioner is tight, the sprockets should be in their correct position.  You will now tighten the rest of the sprockets.

Remove the camshaft lock bar but please read this note: the service manual says to remove the camshaft lock bar before tightening the sprocket bolt but you can tighten the bolt first and then remove the bar so that the shimmed bar holds the camshaft perfectly at TDC.  If you remove the lock bar before tightening the camshaft bolt, it's possible although unlikely, that the camshaft could move a little bit when you tighten the bolt.  The service manual says to remove the bar first because it doesn't want you to torque against the lock bar.  Again, the sprocket counterholding tool should counterhold the sprocket when tightening the camshaft bolt.

Tighten the camshaft sprocket bolt to the factory value 33 ft-lbs but see the caution below!  Remember to counterhold the camshaft sprocket when you tighten the bolt!

CAUTION - Make sure that the tapered conical tip of the camshaft is clean of oil or grease and dry because damage to the threads, tapered camshaft end, or an oily tip can change the force on the taper and result in a spun camshaft sprocket and engine damage!  I use a slightly higher torque spec of about 40 ft-lbs on the camshaft sprocket bolt.  This is only about a 20% increase in torque over the recommended factory value.  The reason why is because there have been reports of spun camshaft sprockets using 33 ft-lbs.  Regardless of the cause (faulty torque wrench, user error, greasy camshaft taper, etc.,), a slightly higher value on this bolt will not damage the camshaft end, sprocket, or the 19mm sprocket bolt, but will add a margin of safety against a spun sprocket.  I believe that this range is a very safe balance between spinning the sprocket and any possible damage to the camshaft or sprocket. 

Here is a breakdown of the components so you can decide for yourself: the bolt, the camshaft threads, the camshaft taper, the sprocket, the belt position.
-The 19mm camshaft sprocket bolt and threads are large compared to the low torque value of 33 ft-lbs and will not be damaged by a small increase in torque value.  Later BEW engines use a torque of 74 ft-lbs on an identical bolt.  Although the camshaft, taper, and setup is different, the bolt is the same.
-The camshaft threads are not brittle enough to be damaged by a 20% increase.  It's made out of steel.  As a matter of opinion from a non-mechanic or engineer, I do not go over 40 although others have tested the camshaft to well over 50 ft-lbs without any damage to the threads when using proper tools and pullers.  Some even recommend 45 ft-lb.  A very high torque could cause some damage.
-The taper is also strong and it's not likely to be damaged or deformed by a small increase.  A much higher torque could deform the taper but a 20% increase isn't going to do any damage.  Of course, whacking the sprocket with a hammer or improper removal can cause damage so always use a puller or the method in the factory service manual.
-There have not been any reports of damage to the sprocket due to a slightly higher torque.
-The belt position on the sprocket is not dictated by the sprocket and is unchanged.

In the end, the torque spec that you want to use is up to you.  Regardless of the cause, I would rather err on the side of caution.  A small increase in torque on this bolt could prevent serious damage and won't damage anything.  More torque isn't better but it's possible that the factory torque specification of 33 ft-lbs is to too low, judging from the rare but catastrophic spun sprocket.  Again, make sure that the taper is clean and dry of any oil or grease.  It's your engine and myturbodiesel is not responsible for any damage as a result of the information found on this website, any non factory or OEM specification or instruction is done at your own risk, etc.,...see the full legal disclaimer in the TOS Agreement.

Remove injection pump locking pin, crankshaft lock bar, and camshaft lock.  Double check for TDC.  Remove tape or plugs from intake manifold.  If you haven't tightened all the bolts, double check them now!

Install the vacuum pump with a new seal at the end of the camshaft so that oil does not get pumped out when you rotate the engine.

Final checks when installing the TDI timing belt

Check that the manual transmission is in neutral gear and rotate the engine twice by hand in running direction (clockwise).  I suggest using the 19mm crankshaft bolt to turn the engine over.  Do not use the starter to rotate the engine.  Turning by hand checks that there is no mechanical interference, that all the small bolts have been tightened, and is a low enough force to not damage anything.

Although it's possible to use the injection pump sprocket to turn over the engine and the sprocket has a lot of teeth engaging the timing belt, do not use the IP sprocket.  The reason is because it has to work against the resistance of the valve springs and if you attached the serpentine belt, it can cause the belt to jump the injection pump sprocket.  Use the crankshaft bolt to turn the engine over but if it's jammed, do not apply any significant force to it since it's a stretch bolt.  You can also use the camshaft sprocket on this exact engine model.  You should feel light resistance as the engine builds compression - let it bleed off and then you can continue turning the engine.  If it seems jammed, double check that the camshaft and injection pump locks are removed and the transmission is in neutral.  A jammed timing belt could also be the valves are hitting the pistons or something left somewhere which is holding the belt.

If you don't know what abnormal resistance is while rotating the engine, you could remove the glow plugs so that there is no engine compression (it all leaks out the glow plug holes).  If you feel abnormal resistance, stop and put the engine back at TDC.  Reinsert the camshaft lock bar and injection pump pin.

After you rotate the engine through 2-4 cycles, put the engine back at TDC and double check that the injection pump pin and camshaft lock bar can go back in.  If they do and the engine rotated normally, the belt is on correctly.  Continue with reinstallation.  If they do not, go back and reset them.

Do not rotate the engine in reverse once the serpentine belt is attached because the various accessories are already worn in one direction.  It's also not great for the injection pump.  Again, do not use the starter to force the engine over.  The whole point of rotating it by hand is so that you don't cause any damage from a loose bolt, timing belt off a tooth, etc.

The rest of installation is the reverse of removal.  Double check all wires, hoses, etc.  Make sure no paper towels are stuffed down the piping.  The harmonic balancer/crankshaft pulley bolts are asymmetrical and the bolt holes will only line up 1 way.  Also check the length of the new bolts against the length of the old bolts.

The (2x 16mm and 2x 18mm) motor mount bolts are torque to yield "stretch bolts" and should be replaced each time they are torqued to their final value.  In other words, do not reuse your old bolts.  Torque specs are below.  If the aluminum motor mount strips at all, stop, remove the bolt, and repair the threads with a helicoil or buy a new motor mount (VW# 038 199 207 h).

Leave the upper timing belt cover off so that you can check the injection pump timing with a VCDS.  Here is the serpentine belt routing.

Torque specs: See 1000q: torque wrench FAQ for tips on torque wrench use.
5mm allen bolts to valve cover: 7 ft-lb (don't overtighten!)
13mm 3 vacuum pump bolts to head: 15 ft-lb
camshaft sprocket bolt: 33 ft-lb, see caution above, you may want to use a higher spec
upper timing belt roller bolt (small): 15 ft-lb
lower timing belt roller nut (small): 16 ft-lb
lower timing belt roller bolt (large):  30 ft-lb +1/4 turn
timing belt tensioner nut: 15 ft-lb
3 injection pump sprocket bolts: 18 ft-lb final torque, should not reuse stretch bolts
4 harmonic balancer bolts: 18 ft-lb (can use medium locktite here)
3 water pump bolts: 11 ft-lb
coolant reservoir and power steering reservoir: 7 ft-lb
16mm engine mount - engine block bracket bolts: 33 ft-lb (the mount pictured to the right)

18mm engine mount-motor mount bolts: 74 ft-lb (red circled bolts to right) - note: original spec was 44 ft lbs + 1/4 turn, suggested new spec is 74 ft lbs to help prevent stripping
16mm motor mount - body/fender/frame bolts: 30 ft-lb + 1/4 turn (blue circled bolts to right)
 motor mount alignment bracket (holds the metal alignment plate) bolts: 18 ft-lb

Note about the single use motor mount stretch bolts: stretch bolts securely clamp through a range of temp and vibration by deforming.  In the event of a frontal crash, I believe that further stress on the bolts + the design of the motor mounts should increase the likelihood that the engine goes underneath the passenger compartment.  For this reason, I do not suggest adding additional bolts to hold the motor mount up.

How to fine tune injection pump timing on TDI engine.

You should now fine tune the injection timing with a Ross Tech VCDS (used to be called VAG-COM).  If you don't have a VCDS but the engine starts, just try to find someone later who has it and use their VCDS to check and fine tune the timing.  The purpose of the locking pin on the injection pump is to ensure that the car will start and is set to the approximate correct timing.  Fine tuning the injection timing will make sure that you are maximizing power, starting, and economy.  If nothing else, it's a good idea to make sure that injection pump timing is set within the normal boundaries of the graph.  Some screenshots from rocketeer928.

Caution: when the engine is running, make sure any bolts/tools/fingers are clear of all belts or radiator fans.  The moving belts or fan can easily rip off your hand or fingers if they get caught in the belts or fan!  See the TOS Agreement for the full legal disclaimer.

Start the car and check engine oil pressure, any abnormal sounds, etc.  Assuming the car is running OK, rev the engine at about 2,500 rpm until the coolant temperature gauge is sitting straight up (ideally at least 85°C or 185°F).  You could also go for a drive until the engine is heated up.  A warm engine is required to check the timing.  

Leave the engine running at idle.  Connect the VCDS cable to the car’s OBD2 port and to your Windows PC.  Start up the VCDS software and you'll see the screen below.  Click “Select”  (circled).

Click “01-Engine”.

After VCDS has established a connection with the ECU, click "Basic Settings - 04".  

You could also click “Meas. Blocks – 08” , then Engine Measuring Block 000 and click “Go!”.  Then click the “Switch to Basic Settings” button.

Click the “TDI Timing” button.  A graph like the ones below will appear and the glowplug light on the instrument panel will flash.  If the graph can't show up or you don't have a genuine VCDS, look at measuring block 000 (shown below).  

If you use data block 000, note block 2 (timing) and 9 (fuel temperature not coolant temp).  When block 9 is reading 110 (this is why you must let the car warm up), then block 2 should show 70.  The values may not correspond to C or F.  It may be an internal value that the ECU translates into temperature.  To be sure, go to "advanced measurement blocks" and check off which values you want to see, there you can see the actual temperature.  Refer to the service manual for the injection timing graph if you can't see the graph through VCDS.

From the drop down menu in the lower right corner, select either “1.9l R4 TDI AGR/AHF/AHL Golf/Jetta (pre 04/1999)” or “1.9l R4 TDI AGR/AHF/AHL/ASV Golf/Jetta (post 03/1999)”, depending on the year of your car.

The horizontal and vertical yellow lines apex is where the timing is set.  Below is the position that the timing happened to land on.  Below the blue line is retarded and above the green line is advanced.  An acceptable range is between the lower red and upper green diagonal lines.

If there are no yellow lines, timing is very retarded.  If there's only a vertical yellow line timing is too advanced (pictured below).

To adjust injection pump timing you must physically adjust the injection pump.  Adjusting the injection pump will change block 2 (the horizontal line if you're looking at the chart).  Always keep the injection pump timing within the boundaries of the timing chart on VCDS.  Slightly advanced timing (above the center line) will result in slightly higher EGT and slightly more engine stress but better fuel economy, power, and easier cold starts.  Slightly advanced timing is preferable to slightly retarded timing although anywhere within the boundaries of the lines on the chart is considered acceptable.

The picture shows timing that is considered acceptable.

To adjust injection pump timing, first shut off the engine.  Take off the upper timing belt cover.  

Slightly loosen the 3x 13mm injection pump sprocket bolts.  Only loosen them enough so that the sprocket can slide under their pressure - if you loosen them fully, the injection pump may move independently of the sprocket due to pressure from the belt and internal forces.  A tip is to loosen them one at a time and then tighten it just barely with a ratchet.  If you loosen them all too much, the sprocket is too free to move and timing may go from way retarded to way advanced.  

Then use a wrench to gently nudge the 22mm center nut that is connected to the injection pump shaft.  Rotating the nut counterclockwise will decrease the block 2 value and retard timing, clockwise will increase block 2 value and advance injection timing.  Refer to your service manual for the exact graph of the injection pump timing envelope for your car.  Do not apply too much pressure to the center injection pump sprocket nut because it's not designed to be removed and you don't want to loosen it!  Then torque the 3x 13  bolts to their final torque of 18 ft lbs  

Note that the service manual says to rotate the injection pump shaft/nut clockwise to advance timing, counterclockwise to retard timing.  Remember that when you move the pulley or shaft in one direction, the relative motion of the shaft is in the other direction.  

Recheck timing by following the earlier steps.  Obviously, when you are adjusting the 3 bolts or injection pump pulley, the engine must be off!

It took rocketeer928 three adjustments to get the timing slightly advanced, as he prefers it (pic from rocketeer928).  Reinstall the upper timing belt cover when satisfied.
